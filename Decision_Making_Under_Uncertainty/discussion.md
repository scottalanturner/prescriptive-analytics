# Week 11 Discussion — The Plan That Assumed Too Much

---

## 📌 What This Discussion Is About

Every optimization model we have built this semester started with a set of inputs treated as known: demand was a number, costs were fixed, capacity was certain. The model did its job — it found the best answer given those inputs.

But this week's readings exposed the assumption underneath all of that work. The McKinsey framework showed that most real decisions live in a world where the future is not Level 1 — where certainty is not actually available. The Society of Actuaries paper laid out what happens when a decision-maker has to choose without knowing which future will arrive. And Professor Thekdi's research raised something harder still: even when you build a model that handles uncertainty honestly, communicating that uncertainty to the people who have to act on it is its own challenge.

This discussion asks you to bring that tension into your own experience.

---

## 💬 Your Discussion Prompt

**Think of a real decision — one you have been part of, observed in your organization, read about, or know from your industry — where the outcome depended heavily on how the future unfolded, and where the plan assumed more certainty than actually existed.**

This does not have to be a decision that failed. It could be one that worked out, one that went sideways, or one that is still playing out. What matters is that the decision was made in an environment where the future was genuinely uncertain — and where that uncertainty shaped everything.

---

## ✅ What to Include in Your Post

Your post should be **400–600 words** and address all four elements below. You do not need headers or a formal structure — write it as a clear, connected narrative.

---

### 1. The Decision and Its Context

Describe the decision. Who was making it? What were they trying to accomplish? What did the outcome depend on?

Be specific. "A company decided how much inventory to carry" is a starting point, but "a regional distributor had to commit to Q4 inventory orders in August without knowing whether a major retail contract would renew" is a decision. The more concrete your description, the more useful the discussion will be.

---

### 2. The Uncertainty That Was Present

Using the framework from **Reading 1** (McKinsey — Strategy Under Uncertainty), identify what *level* of uncertainty best describes this situation:

- **Level 1** — The future was clear enough that a single-point estimate was reasonable
- **Level 2** — There were a small number of distinct possible futures that could have played out
- **Level 3** — There was a wide range of possible outcomes with no clear discrete scenarios
- **Level 4** — The situation was genuinely ambiguous — even the dimensions of uncertainty were unclear

Explain your reasoning. Most decisions you will describe will fall in Level 2 or Level 3.

Then identify: **what was the key uncertainty** that drove this decision? What was the one thing nobody could know for sure, and that mattered most?

---

### 3. What Scenario Thinking Might Have Looked Like

If the decision-maker had applied the kind of structured scenario thinking described in **Reading 2** (SOA — Decision Making Under Uncertain and Risky Situations), what would that have looked like in practice?

- What two or three scenarios would have been worth building?
- How might the decision have looked different under each scenario?
- Is there a strategy that would have performed acceptably across all of them — a robust choice rather than an optimal one?

You are not being asked to build a formal model. Think out loud about whether scenario thinking would have changed what was decided, or only changed how confidently it was decided.

---

### 4. The Communication Challenge

**Reading 3** (Thekdi and Aven) argues that presenting uncertain analysis to decision-makers creates its own credibility problem. When an analyst says "the answer depends on which scenario unfolds," some decision-makers hear honesty — and others hear "you don't actually know."

Reflect on your example: How was uncertainty communicated, if at all? Did the decision-makers want a range of outcomes or a single recommendation? Would presenting multiple scenarios have helped or hurt the decision-making process in that organization?
